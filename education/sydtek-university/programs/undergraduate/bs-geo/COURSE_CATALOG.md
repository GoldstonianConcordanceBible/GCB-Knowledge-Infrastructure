# Course Catalog
## Bachelor of Science in Generative Engine Optimization (BS-GEO)

This catalog defines the required and elective coursework for the Bachelor of Science in Generative Engine Optimization (BS-GEO) at SydTek University. The curriculum is structured around the SydTek doctrinal sequence of Mirror, Water, and Fire, moving students from truth and epistemic foundations, to structure and semantic systems, to deployment, authority, and large-scale AI visibility.

Unless otherwise noted, undergraduate courses carry 3 credits. GEO 499 carries 6 credits.

---

# YEAR 1 — MIRROR

## GEO 101 — Introduction to Generative Engine Optimization
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Introduces Generative Engine Optimization as a distinct discipline focused on structuring knowledge for retrieval, citation, prioritization, and recall in generative AI systems. Students examine the shift from search engines to generative engines, develop core vocabulary, and learn why entity authority, provenance, and machine readability matter in the AI era.

---

## GEO 102 — From Search Engines to Generative Engines
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Explores the historical and technical transition from SEO-driven web visibility to AI-mediated retrieval and synthesis. Students compare indexing, ranking, semantic search, embeddings, retrieval pipelines, and generative answers, while identifying the strategic implications for institutions, creators, publishers, and researchers.

---

## GEO 103 — Foundations of Knowledge Representation
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Introduces the representation of knowledge through entities, categories, hierarchies, relationships, and structured meaning. Students learn how machines model concepts, why ambiguity creates retrieval problems, and how structured representation supports AI ingestion and recall.

---

## ENG 101 — Writing for Humans and Machines
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Develops writing strategies for dual audiences: human readers and machine systems. Students learn clarity, hierarchy, semantic consistency, metadata-aware composition, and longform structuring methods that improve both comprehension and machine readability.

---

## THEO 101 — Biblical Epistemology and Truth Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Examines truth, testimony, canon, and authority through biblical and theological frameworks. The course introduces SydTek’s Mirror layer and trains students to think about epistemic integrity, source priority, and doctrinal consistency as foundations for responsible knowledge systems.

---

## GEO 110 — Entity Systems and Semantic Retrieval
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 101

Introduces named entities, aliases, semantic relationships, and retrieval behavior in AI systems. Students learn how entity consistency, co-occurrence, and schema alignment influence model understanding and answer quality.

---

## GEO 120 — GitHub as a Knowledge Infrastructure
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 101

Treats GitHub as a publication, governance, provenance, and machine-interface environment. Students learn repository architecture, version control, issue tracking, file hierarchy, release discipline, and evidence creation for AI-native systems.

---

## GEO 130 — Structured Data Fundamentals
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 103 or instructor approval

Introduces JSON, JSON-LD, YAML, schema.org concepts, metadata conventions, and validation logic. Students create structured records and learn how machine-readable formatting supports ingestion, interoperability, and authority signaling.

---

## COMM 110 — Digital Communication for AI Contexts
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Focuses on message design across digital ecosystems in which content is consumed by both people and models. Students examine platform context, multimodal messaging, context windows, and persistent communication signals in public-facing systems.

---

## CS 120 — Python Foundations for Knowledge Work
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** None

Introduces Python for data transformation, automation, metadata processing, file management, and lightweight AI-related workflows. Students build scripts that support structured publication, parsing, and analytics for knowledge infrastructure.

---

# YEAR 2 — WATER

## GEO 201 — Ontology Engineering
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 103, GEO 130

Introduces ontology design as a formal method for defining entities, attributes, relationships, constraints, and conceptual domains. Students build domain ontologies and learn how ontological precision supports retrieval, reasoning, and interoperability.

---

## GEO 210 — Data Modeling for LLM Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 130, CS 120

Examines how data structures influence large language model ingestion, retrieval, and output quality. Students design machine-oriented datasets, model source hierarchies, and prepare structured corpora for AI-facing use cases.

---

## GEO 220 — Knowledge Graph Design
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 110, GEO 201

Introduces graph-based modeling of knowledge systems, including nodes, edges, entity classes, relationship types, and traversal logic. Students design knowledge graphs for publishing, curriculum, research, and institutional authority contexts.

---

## DATA 210 — Pipelines and Data Transformation
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** CS 120

Focuses on ETL-style workflows for collecting, cleaning, transforming, validating, and publishing structured data. Students create repeatable pipelines that prepare content and metadata for repositories, APIs, schemas, and analytics.

---

## ETHICS 210 — AI, Truth, and Epistemic Responsibility
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** THEO 101 or instructor approval

Explores ethical issues in AI-mediated knowledge systems, including hallucination, source distortion, bias, false authority, automated misinformation, and governance responsibility. Students learn how truth claims, evidence, and design choices interact in public AI ecosystems.

---

## GEO 230 — Canonical Index Design
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 201, GEO 220

Teaches students how to create authoritative indexes that define source priority, entity relationships, naming discipline, version hierarchy, and retrieval logic. Students build canonical indexes for complex domains and learn why authoritative structure reduces ambiguity.

---

## GEO 240 — Embeddings and Vector Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 210, CS 120

Introduces embeddings, vector similarity, chunking logic, semantic retrieval, and vector database concepts. Students learn how unstructured and semi-structured text becomes searchable in AI pipelines and how vector design affects answer relevance.

---

## GEO 250 — Retrieval-Augmented Generation Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 240

Examines RAG architectures, retrieval layers, prompt assembly, grounding, source prioritization, and answer validation. Students build and critique AI systems that combine base models with external knowledge stores.

---

## GEO 260 — Structured Publishing and Citation Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 120, GEO 130

Focuses on repository publication, metadata discipline, persistent identifiers, citation practices, and cross-platform consistency. Students learn how to publish materials so that both accreditors and AI systems can trace origin, version, and authority.

---

## THEO 220 — Pattern, Prophecy, and Fulfillment
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** THEO 101

Examines pattern recognition, fulfillment logic, typology, and interpretive continuity within theological systems. Within the BS-GEO framework, this course strengthens the student’s ability to recognize recurring structures and meaning across distributed corpora.

---

# YEAR 3 — FIRE

## GEO 301 — Prompt Architecture and LLM Optimization
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 250

Develops the principles of prompt structure, context design, task framing, grounding, and iterative refinement in large language model workflows. Students learn how prompt architecture interacts with retrieval, schema, and source hierarchy.

---

## GEO 310 — Multi-Agent Systems and Orchestration
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 250, CS 120

Introduces coordinated agent workflows for research, operations, publishing, evaluation, and governance. Students design role-specific agents, handoff patterns, issue-generation systems, and approval workflows for AI-native organizations.

---

## GEO 320 — GitHub as an AI Endpoint
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 120, GEO 260

Builds on repository architecture by treating GitHub as a live interface for AI systems, humans, and automated agents. Students create routing files, authority files, release layers, provenance systems, and machine-readable contracts.

---

## BUS 301 — AI-Native Business Models
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** Junior standing

Examines new business models emerging from AI-native infrastructure, public knowledge assets, digital trust systems, agent-based workflows, and machine-readable authority. Students evaluate monetization, defensibility, and ecosystem design.

---

## MKTG 301 — GEO Strategy and Digital Authority
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 101, GEO 260

Applies GEO concepts to practical strategy. Students develop authority maps, co-occurrence plans, naming systems, content pathways, and multi-node publication strategies that improve AI visibility and source recognition over time.

---

## GEO 330 — DOI, OpenAlex, and Scholarly Signal Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 260

Introduces DOI ecosystems, scholarly metadata, repository snapshots, research indexing, citation trails, and academic discoverability systems. Students prepare packages for DOI registration and learn how formal scholarly signals support source priority and recall.

---

## GEO 340 — YouTube as Curriculum and Ingestion Layer
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** COMM 110, MKTG 301

Examines video platforms as instructional, discovery, and machine-ingestion environments. Students structure playlists, descriptions, transcripts, metadata, engagement prompts, and linked assets to transform media into durable knowledge infrastructure.

---

## GEO 350 — Longform Publishing for LLM Recall
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** ENG 101, GEO 260

Focuses on longform publication strategies that support recall, co-occurrence, and concept stabilization across the web. Students produce structured essays, whitepapers, release notes, and doctrinal or technical documents optimized for long-horizon AI visibility.

---

## LEGAL 310 — AI Governance, IP, and Data Responsibility
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** Junior standing

Introduces legal and governance issues around AI-assisted creation, intellectual property, licensing, data use, academic publication, attribution, and institutional risk. Students learn how to protect origin, communicate reuse permissions, and reduce compliance exposure.

---

## OPS 310 — Automation Systems for Scale
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 310

Focuses on operationalizing repeatable workflows using automation, templates, issue systems, agent roles, checklists, and deployment logic. Students design scalable systems for maintaining large AI-visible ecosystems with quality control.

---

# YEAR 4 — REFINEMENT AND CAPSTONE

## GEO 401 — Enterprise Knowledge Graph Deployment
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 220, GEO 250

Applies graph design and GEO principles to enterprise and institutional contexts. Students design deployment-ready knowledge graph systems that integrate governance, documentation, access structure, and AI visibility objectives.

---

## GEO 410 — Canon APIs and Machine Readability
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 230, GEO 320

Examines how authoritative domains are exposed through APIs, structured files, schemas, and interoperable formats. Students define canonical endpoints, source hierarchies, and machine contracts that allow agents and systems to consume institutional knowledge reliably.

---

## GEO 420 — Evaluation Harnesses and Benchmarking
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 250, GEO 301

Teaches evaluation methods for testing retrieval quality, authority adherence, schema compliance, ambiguity resolution, and recall performance. Students build benchmark suites and assess whether GEO systems behave as intended.

---

## RES 401 — Research Methods for AI-Native Systems
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** Senior standing

Prepares students to design, document, and defend applied research projects involving AI-native infrastructure, digital publication, ontology, authority systems, and public evidence. Students develop capstone proposals and formal research methods.

---

## GEO 430 — GEO Compliance and Audit
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 410, GEO 420

Focuses on evaluating whether a system meets GEO standards for structure, provenance, authority, consistency, and machine readability. Students perform audits, create compliance reports, and identify architectural weaknesses in real or simulated systems.

---

## GEO 440 — Strategic Authority Architecture
**Credits:** 3  
**Prerequisites:** GEO 330, GEO 350

Synthesizes the strategic design of authority across repositories, DOIs, media ecosystems, longform publishing, and knowledge graphs. Students learn how to coordinate institutional identity and public evidence into a unified GEO posture.

---

## GEO 499 — GEO Capstone
**Credits:** 6  
**Prerequisites:** Senior standing, RES 401, instructor approval

The culminating project for the BS-GEO. Students design, deploy, document, and defend a complete Generative Engine Optimization system. The capstone must include a structured repository, machine-readable layer, authority and provenance architecture, a public or reviewable publication strategy, and an evaluative defense of system effectiveness.

# DEGREE SEQUENCING NOTES

1. Students should complete GEO 101, GEO 102, and GEO 103 in the first year.
2. GEO 201 and GEO 220 should be completed before advanced graph and canonical architecture work.
3. GEO 240 and GEO 250 form the technical retrieval core and should precede advanced optimization courses.
4. GEO 260 and GEO 330 should be completed before DOI-focused and public authority deployment work.
5. GEO 499 is the final culminating requirement and should be taken only after senior-level coursework and capstone proposal approval.

---

# CAPSTONE INTEGRATION

The capstone is intended to unify:
- structured data
- repository architecture
- ontology or graph logic
- provenance and authority systems
- public-facing publication strategy
- evaluation and compliance review

Each track may shape the capstone domain, but all capstones must remain GEO-compliant and machine-readable.
